Room First

Build Around the Listening Position

The most useful studio layout begins with the relationship between the listener, speakers, desk, room boundaries, and intended work.

A studio used for mixing has different priorities from a room used mainly for editing, casual recording, music production, podcasting, or entertainment.

Before selecting panels, review the room dimensions, speaker distance, seating position, desk depth, doors, windows, furniture, available wall area, ceiling height, and whether the layout can be adjusted.

Geometry

Room proportions, symmetry, boundaries, and source-to-listener distances influence the starting layout.

Equipment

Speaker size, placement limits, desk reflections, screens, stands, subwoofers, and instruments all affect the plan.

Use

Critical listening, production, recording, streaming, editing, and shared-room use require different compromises.

Listening Position

The listening position influences symmetry, distance from boundaries, speaker angle, stereo image, desk relationship, and where low-frequency peaks or nulls may be experienced.

  • Begin with a symmetrical left-to-right relationship where the room allows it.
  • Avoid assuming the exact centre of the room is automatically the best listening location.
  • Adjust the listener and speaker positions before relying on treatment alone.

Sound Paths

Direct Sound and Early Reflections

The listener receives sound directly from the speakers and shortly afterward from nearby surfaces such as side walls, the desk, ceiling, front wall, and equipment.

These early arrivals may influence clarity, imaging, tonal balance, and the ability to judge spatial detail. Their effect depends on timing, level, direction, frequency, and the room’s intended use.

The common mirror method can help locate geometric side-wall reflection points, but it does not measure frequency response, decay, bass behaviour, or the complete effect of a surface.

Studio Treatment Direction

Treat the Important Relationships

Each treatment zone should connect to an audible or measurable priority rather than being added only because a wall is empty.

01

Listening Position

Small movements can change the low-frequency response and the relationship between direct and reflected sound. Positioning should be explored before assuming treatment alone will solve every issue.

  • Review symmetry and boundary distances.
  • Compare several practical listener positions.
  • Keep the speaker-to-listener relationship consistent.
02

First Reflections

Side-wall and ceiling treatment can help manage strong early reflections, but panel thickness, size, placement, spacing, and the existing room balance all matter.

  • Locate reflection zones from the actual listening position.
  • Consider both side walls and the ceiling relationship.
  • Avoid treating one side differently without a clear reason.
03

Low-Frequency Control

Bass problems usually need greater treatment depth and careful positioning. Thin decorative panels should not be expected to provide the same low-frequency effect as deeper systems.

  • Use available corners and boundaries strategically.
  • Compare depth and tested performance, not appearance alone.
  • Keep expectations realistic in very small rooms.
04

Rear-Wall Energy

The rear wall may be close to the listener in a compact studio, making treatment depth, available space, storage, and the balance between absorption and scattering important.

  • Consider the listener-to-rear-wall distance.
  • Use deeper treatment where space allows.
  • Integrate storage only when it does not obstruct the priority.
05

Desk and Equipment

Large desks, screens, racks, and nearby equipment create reflection surfaces and can restrict speaker positioning. Their height, angle, and distance should be part of the acoustic layout.

  • Keep speakers clear of unnecessary obstructions.
  • Review desk height, depth, and surface area.
  • Use stands or supports suited to the speaker design.

Planning Information

Prepare the Studio Details

Useful room information makes it easier to separate positioning, reflection, bass, recording, and sound-isolation concerns.

Request a Studio Plan
01

Measure the Room

Record length, width, height, alcoves, sloped surfaces, open doorways, connected spaces, and ceiling details.

02

Map the Layout

Show the desk, listening seat, speakers, subwoofer, screens, instruments, storage, doors, windows, and current treatment.

03

Describe the Equipment

Include speaker type and size, stands, desk dimensions, subwoofer use, microphone position, and typical monitoring level.

04

Name the Main Problem

Explain whether the concern involves imaging, echo, harshness, unclear recordings, uneven bass, neighbour noise, or placement uncertainty.

05

Share the Visual Direction

Provide preferred colours, fabrics, timber tones, panel shapes, visible finishes, and whether treatment should be discreet or expressive.

06

Define the Limits

Include budget context, rental restrictions, mounting rules, available depth, deadlines, and whether specialist installation is possible.
