Performance and Design

Choose Panels for a Specific Job

An acoustic panel should be considered as part of a room strategy rather than a decorative object with assumed performance.

Panel depth, core, covering, backing, air gap, dimensions, mounting, location, surface coverage, and tested assembly all influence how the product behaves.

Before choosing a style, define the room use, audible problem, priority frequency range, available wall or ceiling area, mounting limits, visual direction, safety requirements, and whether deeper low-frequency treatment is needed.

Purpose

Speech control, first reflections, rear-wall energy, ceiling reflections, bass treatment, and visual zoning require different approaches.

Construction

Core density, thickness, frame, fabric, perforation, backing, cavity, edge detail, and mounting affect the complete assembly.

Integration

Colour, scale, rhythm, shape, seams, timber, lighting, furniture, and camera composition can be coordinated with acoustic priorities.

Side-Wall Panels

Side-wall panels may help manage early reflections between a source and listener or microphone, but their useful position depends on the actual geometry and intended room use.

Effective Depth

Panel Thickness and Air Gaps

Porous acoustic treatment interacts with sound through its complete depth, material structure, surface covering, backing, mounting, and distance from the boundary.

A deeper panel or suitable air gap may extend useful absorption lower in frequency compared with a very thin surface-mounted product, but there is no universal thickness that suits every purpose.

Published test data should be reviewed for the complete product and mounting condition. A fabric colour, foam shape, timber face, or core name alone does not define performance.

Panel Selection Direction

Match the Panel to the Purpose

Panel appearance, placement, thickness, mounting, and verified performance should be reviewed together rather than as separate product features.

01

Broadband Panels

Broadband treatment is commonly used for reflections across a useful range, but actual performance depends on the core, thickness, covering, mounting, air gap, and tested assembly.

  • Choose depth according to the room goal and available space.
  • Use acoustically suitable covering materials and complete-product data.
  • Coordinate panel area and placement with the source and listener relationship.
02

Ceiling Clouds

Ceiling treatment can address strong overhead reflections where wall area is limited or the source, listener, microphone, and desk create an important vertical path.

  • Check panel weight, fixings, substrate, clearance, and suspension method.
  • Coordinate lighting, sprinklers, detectors, ventilation, and electrical services.
  • Use qualified installation where required by weight, height, or building conditions.
03

Bass Treatment

Low-frequency control generally requires more depth and volume than thin surface treatment. Corners, boundaries, rear walls, ceilings, or purpose-built systems may be considered.

  • Review room dimensions, source position, listening area, and available depth.
  • Compare tested frequency range rather than relying on the label “bass trap.”
  • Keep expectations realistic where room volume and treatment space are limited.
04

Slats and Architectural Systems

Slatted and perforated systems can provide a strong visual finish, but their acoustic behaviour varies with slat dimensions, spacing, backing, cavity, insulation, and complete tested build-up.

  • Review the full system rather than the visible timber face alone.
  • Coordinate module sizes, joints, corners, sockets, doors, and furniture.
  • Confirm fire, material, maintenance, and installation information.
05

Freestanding Treatment

Movable treatment can support rented rooms, temporary recording areas, changing layouts, shared spaces, and situations where permanent mounting is restricted.

  • Consider stability, floor area, circulation, storage, and safe movement.
  • Position panels according to the active source and microphone or listener.
  • Use reversible systems without blocking exits, ventilation, or required access.
Material Comparisons

Compare the Complete Assembly

Surface appearance is only one part of a panel. Review the complete product, mounting condition, tested data, installation, maintenance, and room purpose.

Material 01

Fabric-Wrapped Panels

Flexible colour and edge options can suit many interiors, while the core, covering, thickness, frame, and mounting determine the complete product.

Material 02

Felt Systems

Felt can create thin, lightweight, patterned surfaces, but useful acoustic range depends on thickness, layering, backing, cavity, and tested construction.

Material 03

Timber Slats

Slat spacing, timber dimensions, backing, cavity depth, insulation, module joints, and tested build-up influence the final result.

Material 04

Perforated Faces

Hole pattern, open area, face material, backing, cavity, insulation, and mounting determine whether a perforated system suits the intended range.
